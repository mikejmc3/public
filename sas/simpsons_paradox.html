Simpson's Paradox

Simpson's Paradox refers to a phenomenon in which a trend observed at an aggregate level actually reverses when the data are divided into relevant subgroups. When modeling nested data that organizes individual data elements into one or more levels of a hierarchy (e.g., employees nested within teams, which are nested within departments, which are nested within divisions, and so on), it is critical to remain mindful of the potential impact that "nestedness" may have on any conclusions that may be drawn from the data. Ignoring hierarchies in the data may not simply result in less optimal model fit; resulting conclusions may be fundamentally wrong!

Fit a Simple Model that Ignores Data Groups

Let's fit an ordinary least squares (OLS) regression model that predicts values of the target (or dependent) variable y from values of the input (or independent) variable x, ignoring the group variable.

* We will use ODS SELECT to display only specific output;
ods select FitStatistics ParameterEstimates FitPlot;
proc reg data=work.simpson;
    model y=x;
run;
quit;

Under a simple OLS regression model like this, a given observation $i$ of the target variable y ($y_{i}$) is calculated as follows.

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + e_i $$

Using the coefficients estimated from this model, we calculate the prediction formula as follows.

$$ \hat{y} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x $$

$$ \hat{y} = 5.363 + 0.228 x $$

The estimated intercept ($\hat{\beta}_0 = 5.363$) and slope ($\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.228$) are both fixed effects in this model, meaning they do not vary for any observations in our data set. Our interpretation of this model's slope leads us to conclude that a 1-unit increase in x corresponds to a 0.228 unit increase in the predicted value of y.

Is this an accurate representation of the relationship of x and y? What happens if we account for the fact that our x and y values are nested within group?

Fit a Multilevel Model

Let's use a slightly more complex model to account for the fact that our data are nested within levels of the group variable. Now we will fit a multilevel model (sometimes referred to as a hierarchical linear model, or in SAS terminology, a mixed model). In this example, we will specify the intercept as a random effect (i.e., allow intercepts to vary across values of group) and the slope will remain a fixed effect (i.e., will not vary across group). 

ods select ModelInfo ConvergenceStatus CovParms FitStatistics SolutionF SolutionR;
proc mixed data=work.simpson;
    class group;
    model y=x / solution outp=work.mixed_pred;  * Save predicted values to an output data table;
    random int / sub=group solution;
run;
sas

For individual data observation $i$ within group level $j$, we can express the prediction in the following two-level model.

Level 1 Equation

$$ y_{ij} = \beta_{0j} + \beta_{1j} x_{ij} + e_{ij} $$

Level 2 Equation

Because we are allowing intercepts to vary across levels of group, but we are keeping slopes fixed, we write the level 2 model as follows. Notice how the intercept ($\beta_{0j}$) has an additional term because it is a random effect in this model.

$$ \beta_{0j} = \gamma_{00} + u_{0j} $$

$$ \beta_{1j} = \gamma_{10} $$

Reduced-Form Equation

The reduced-form equation can be written as follows. I have reordered the equation to list all of the fixed components first, followed by the random components.

$$ y_{ij} = \gamma_{00} + \gamma_{10} x_{ij} + u_{0j} + e_{ij} $$

Residual Intraclass Correlation

In this model, we have partitioned our variance into two components: between groups and within groups. Variance at level 1 (within groups) is distributed as follows.

$$ e_{ij} \sim N(0, \sigma^{2}_{e}). $$

Variance at level 2 (between groups) is distributed as follows.

$$ u_{0j} \sim N(0, \tau_{00}). $$

Using the values found in the Covariance Parameter Estimates table above, we can calculate the residual intraclass correlation (ICC) as:

$$ ICC = {{\hat{\tau}_{00}} \over {\hat{\tau}_{00} + \hat{\sigma}^{2}_{e}}}, $$

which is:

$$ ICC = {{3492.98} \over {3492.98 + 9.37}} = 0.997. $$

Here, the residual ICC tells us that the proportion of total residual variability in y (controlling for predictors) that is between groups in this example data set is 0.997.

Revisiting the Slope

Recall that the estimated slope from the simple OLS regression model that ignored groups was estimated as 0.228, indicating a positive relationship between x and y. Looking at the Solution for Fixed Effects table in the multilevel model output, we see that the slope is estimated to be -0.260 when we allow intercepts to vary across levels of group, which tells us that the relationship between x and y is negative when we allow intercepts to vary across levels of group.

Let's look at the observed and predicted values from the multilevel model that accounts for group.

proc sort data=work.mixed_pred out=work.plot_mixed_pred;
    by group x;
run;

proc sgplot data=work.plot_mixed_pred;
    scatter x=x y=y / group=group;
    series x=x y=pred / group=group;
    xaxis grid;
    yaxis grid;
run;
sas

Conclusion

This simple demonstration illustrates that ignoring hierarchies in data may not simply reduce model fit, but lead the researcher to reach a false conclusion regarding the nature of the relationship between the predictor(s) and the outcome.
